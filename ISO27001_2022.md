# ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A — New and Merged Controls

## Overview
<!-- meta: section_id=OVERVIEW, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=false, cross_refs=[], keywords=["ISMS","Annex A","revision","confidentiality","integrity","availability"] -->

Information is a valuable asset that needs to be protected against unwanted access, theft,
and manipulation by suitable mechanisms and structures. The international standard
ISO/IEC 27001 supports organizations in implementing robust Information Security
Management Systems (ISMS). Annex A of the standard defines a comprehensive and
flexible catalog of proven information security measures ("controls").

As part of the third revision of the standard in 2022, Annex A was fundamentally revised
and expanded by adding 11 new controls. The revision also merged 24 controls from the
2013 edition into combined, updated controls.

The ISO 27001 standard has been extensively revised and republished on October 25, 2022.
In addition to the change from the High-Level Structure to the Harmonized Structure, the
revised normative Annex A reduced the total control count from 114 controls in 14 sections
(2013 edition) to 93 controls in 4 security categories (2022 edition), while adding 11
genuinely new controls.

Since May 1, 2024, certification according to the updated 2022 version is mandatory.
Existing certificates issued against the 2013 edition cease to be valid at the end of
October 31, 2025.

## Section 1 — Structure of the New Annex A
<!-- meta: section_id=S1, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=true, has_table=true, cross_refs=[], keywords=["organizational controls","people controls","physical controls","technological controls","A.5","A.6","A.7","A.8"] -->

Annex A of ISO/IEC 27001:2022 is organized into four security categories:

| Section | Category | Total Controls | Extant (carried from 2013) | New in 2022 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A.5 | Organizational controls | 37 | 34 | 3 |
| A.6 | People controls | 8 | 8 | 0 |
| A.7 | Physical controls | 14 | 13 | 1 |
| A.8 | Technological controls | 34 | 27 | 7 |
| **Total** | | **93** | **82** | **11** |

This compression to 93 controls (from 114 in the 2013 edition) is the result of two
simultaneous changes: the introduction of 11 new information security controls, and the
merging of 24 controls from the 2013 edition into updated, consolidated controls in the
2022 edition. The 24 merged controls each combine two to four controls from the previous
edition to better reflect the current cybersecurity and information security environment.

A key outcome of comparing an organization's risk treatment measures against this catalog
(per requirement 6.1.3 c) is the Statement of Applicability (SoA).

## Section 3 — A.5.23 Information Security for Use of Cloud Services
<!-- meta: section_id=S3, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=false, cross_refs=[], keywords=["cloud","cloud service provider","CSA","exit strategy","service agreement"], applies_to=["it_security","compliance_officer","vendor_reviewer"] -->

### Purpose
Control A.5.23 defines and manages information security by systematically implementing
processes for acquiring, using, managing, and decommissioning cloud services. The control
addresses the division of responsibility between the cloud service provider and the
organization using the cloud, and requires a topic-specific approach so that cloud service
policies are tailored to individual business functions.

### Organizational obligations

- Define all relevant requirements, selection criteria, and scope associated with using a
  cloud service.
- Coordinate roles and responsibilities for internal use and management, including the
  management of security measures with the cloud service provider.
- Clarify in detail the provision, use, and review of security measures provided by the
  provider, particularly when using multiple cloud services from different providers.
- Implement appropriate reactive procedures (e.g. incident management) for the potential
  occurrence of security incidents with cloud services.
- Continuously monitor, review, and evaluate cloud usage to prevent incidents.
- Define processes for changing or discontinuing the use of a service, including explicit
  exit strategies for cloud services.

### Service agreement requirements

Cloud service agreements are often predefined and non-negotiable, so organizations should
review them carefully against operational requirements for confidentiality, integrity,
availability, and information processing. The contract should address:

- Cloud solutions based on recognized architectures, with secure access controls and
  protection against malware.
- Processing and storage of sensitive information restricted to approved locations or a
  specific jurisdiction.
- Provider support for security incidents and forensic collection of digital evidence,
  including obligations on any external sub-providers involved.
- Provider availability for support and service requests for a reasonable period after
  service termination, including secure provision of backup copies, configuration files,
  source code, and organization-owned data upon request or at termination.
- Provider obligation to notify the organization of relevant changes — technical
  infrastructure, sub-contractors, or geographical/legal jurisdiction.

## Section 4 — A.5.30 ICT Readiness for Business Continuity
<!-- meta: section_id=S4, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=false, cross_refs=[], keywords=["business continuity","BCM","BIA","RTO","RPO","disruption"], applies_to=["it_security","compliance_officer"] -->

### Purpose
Control A.5.30 requires organizations to ensure the availability of ICT even during a
disruption, maintaining the integrity and availability of information and business
processes before, during, and after a business interruption. This requires comprehensive
Business Continuity Management (BCM) underpinned by a thorough Business Impact Analysis
(BIA) that identifies key processes, their interdependencies, and their criticality.

The BIA process must define impact types and impact criteria from which priority
operational activities are derived. Each prioritized activity is assigned a binding
Recovery Time Objective (RTO) and the necessary resources. Recovery Point Objectives
(RPOs) and their intervals must also be defined for prioritized ICT resources.

### Implementation requirements

- Ensure the organizational structure is adequately prepared for a potential incident
  (skilled personnel, containment, reactive measures).
- Draw up binding ICT continuity plans — including response and recovery procedures — in
  consultation with top management, describing in detail how the organization will handle
  an ICT service disruption.
- Regularly evaluate continuity plans through exercises and tests.
- Specify, within the continuity plan, the RTO of each prioritized ICT service and the RPO
  of each prioritized ICT resource, along with the recovery procedure for each.

## Section 5 — Detection and Prevention (A.5.7, A.8.16, A.8.23)
<!-- meta: section_id=S5, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=false, cross_refs=[], keywords=["threat intelligence","monitoring activities","web filtering","anomaly detection","IDS","IPS"], applies_to=["it_security"] -->

### Purpose
Organizational control A.5.7 (Threat intelligence) together with technological controls
A.8.16 (Monitoring activities) and A.8.23 (Web filtering) help organizations prevent and
detect cyber attacks early. According to IBM's Cost of a Data Breach Report 2023, it took
an average of 204 days to detect an attack in 2023 and a further 73 days to contain it.

### A.5.7 Threat intelligence

Threat information must be systematically collected and analyzed across three levels:

- **Strategic** — insight into the changing threat landscape (attack types, actors),
  sourced from national/international government agencies (e.g. BSI, ENISA, NIST) or
  non-profit organizations and relevant forums.
- **Tactical** — information about attackers' methods, tools, and technologies.
- **Operational** — detailed information on specific attacks, including technical
  indicators.

Threat intelligence should be integrated into the risk management process and used to
test and update cyber defenses (firewalls, anti-malware, etc.). Data quality from A.5.7
directly impacts the effectiveness of A.8.16 and A.8.23.

### A.8.16 Monitoring activities

Organizations must define normal/expected behavior for networks, systems, and
applications in order to identify deviations as anomalies (overload, unauthorized access
attempts, unplanned termination of processes). Effective monitoring requires a clean,
transparent IT/OT infrastructure. The monitoring system should cover:

- Incoming and outgoing network, system, and application traffic.
- Access to systems, servers, network devices, monitoring systems, and critical
  applications.
- System and network configuration files at the administrative or business-critical
  level.
- Logs from security tools — antivirus, IDS, IPS, web filters, firewalls, data leakage
  prevention.
- Event logs of system and network activity.
- Verification of integrity and authorization of executable code on a system.
- Resource usage — processor performance, disk capacity, memory usage, bandwidth.

### A.8.23 Web filtering

This control protects systems from malware intrusion via the internet and prevents
access to unauthorized web resources. Organizations should establish rules for safe and
appropriate use of online resources, including mandatory access restrictions on unwanted
or inappropriate websites and web applications. Organizations should specifically block
access to known command-and-control servers and websites identified as malicious under
A.5.7, those providing illegal content, or those with an information-upload function
(except where there is a legitimate business reason).

## Section 6 — A.8.9 Configuration Management
<!-- meta: section_id=S6, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=false, cross_refs=[], keywords=["configuration management","misconfiguration","change management","Infrastructure as Code"], applies_to=["it_security"] -->

### Purpose
Control A.8.9 stipulates that security configurations of hardware, software, services,
and networks must be systematically defined and continuously documented, implemented,
monitored, and checked. In a 2022 Arctic Wolf study, 81% of security managers stated that
vulnerabilities and unknown misconfigurations cause the most serious security problems
in their infrastructures.

### Implementation requirements

- Enforce defined configurations for both new and existing systems throughout their
  entire lifecycle, with appropriate roles, responsibilities, and change-management
  procedures.
- Define standard configuration templates based on best-practice guidelines, required
  protection levels, and internal security requirements; review and update templates
  regularly.
- Deactivate unnecessary or insecure identities and minimize identities with extensive
  access rights.
- Document all configurations and changes in configuration databases or templates,
  including owner, last change, template version, and relationships to other asset
  configurations.
- Use system management tools (maintenance programs, remote support, enterprise
  management tools, backup/recovery software) to verify configuration settings, assess
  password strength, and evaluate activities performed.
- When actuals deviate from target templates, respond with automatic enforcement of the
  target configuration or manual analysis and remediation. Automated approaches such as
  Infrastructure as Code are often more effective than manual processes.

## Section 7 — Information Protection (A.8.10, A.8.11, A.8.12)
<!-- meta: section_id=S7, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=false, cross_refs=[], keywords=["information deletion","data masking","pseudonymization","anonymization","data leakage prevention","DLP"], applies_to=["it_security","compliance_officer"] -->

### Purpose
Controls A.8.10–A.8.12 enhance protection of sensitive data by enforcing data deletion,
masking sensitive information, and continuously monitoring data and information flows.
Per the German "Wirtschaftsschutz 2023" study, 41% of surveyed companies had already been
affected by theft of digital data, with another 29% strongly suspecting it.

### A.8.10 Information deletion

Deleting data no longer required prevents unwanted disclosure. Required practices:

- Use compliant deletion procedures — electronic overwriting or cryptographic deletion.
- Document or make verifiable any deletion process performed by a service provider.
- Contractually require deletion of data by third parties storing data on the
  organization's behalf, including for the period after service termination.
- Establish dedicated systems for secure destruction of information.
- Delete obsolete versions, copies, and temporary files regardless of storage location.
- Use only approved and secure deletion software, or approved/certified disposal service
  providers.
- Use disposal mechanisms appropriate to the storage media.
- For cloud services, verify the legitimacy of deletion procedures offered, or request
  deletion directly from the cloud provider.
- When devices are returned or disposed of, remove all storage capacity, use internal
  factory-reset functions, or destroy the device.
- Document deletion procedures for traceability, proportionate to data sensitivity.

### A.8.11 Data masking

Data masking, anonymization, or (weaker) pseudonymization should be used to protect
sensitive information such as personal data — hiding personal data, disguising true
data values, and hiding links to other information. Users must only see the data they
genuinely need; remaining data in a dataset should be masked, and in some cases made
unrecognizable. Key considerations: strength of masking, access granted to the data,
data-use agreements, and prohibition of data matching for re-identification or for
tracking the provision and receipt of data.

### A.8.12 Data leakage prevention

This control prevents and detects data leaks across all systems, networks, and devices
that process, store, or transmit sensitive information. Organizations should:

- Identify and categorize sensitive information.
- Monitor external data transfer channels.
- Identify and monitor sensitive information at risk of unauthorized disclosure (e.g. in
  unstructured data on a user's system).
- Detect disclosure of confidential data (e.g. uploads to untrusted third-party cloud
  services, or transmission via email).
- Block user actions or network transfers that would expose critical information (e.g.
  prevent database records from being copied to a spreadsheet).
- Review and, where necessary, restrict users' permissions to copy, paste, or upload
  data externally.
- For particularly sensitive information, consider measures to confuse attackers — e.g.
  substituting false information or using honeypots.
- Balance the level of surveillance used against applicable privacy, data protection,
  employment, and telecommunications laws.

## Section 8 — A.8.28 Secure Coding
<!-- meta: section_id=S8, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=false, cross_refs=[], keywords=["secure coding","SAST","OWASP","SEI CERT","SBOM","least privilege"], applies_to=["it_security"] -->

### Purpose
Control A.8.28 proactively secures software during development by establishing a robust
minimum security baseline through procedural rules, minimizing potential security
vulnerabilities. The rules apply holistically to in-house code as well as third-party and
open-source software. Reference standards include the OWASP Foundation's Secure Coding
Practices, the SEI CERT Coding Standards, and the German BSI's "Catalog of Measures for
the Security of Web Applications."

### Implementation requirements

- Establish organization-specific secure-coding expectations and principles during
  planning and pre-coding; document and avoid known/historical coding mistakes.
- Configure development tools to enforce secure code creation in IDEs on a rule-based
  basis.
- Ensure developer qualification in secure programming standards, architectures, and
  threat modeling.
- Apply secure coding practices in the ongoing development process — pair programming,
  refactoring, peer review.
- Consistently prevent insecure design techniques such as hard-coded passwords.
- Document code and permanently check it via static application security testing (SAST).
- Assess the attack surface, implement the Least-Privilege-Principle, and analyze the
  most common programming errors with documentation of their elimination before the
  software goes into operation.
- After deployment, apply secure updates and code checks for known vulnerabilities;
  document errors and suspected attacks for prompt remediation.
- Prevent unauthorized access to source code via appropriate configuration management
  tooling.
- Carefully evaluate external code components (e.g. open-source) before use, and manage,
  update, and maintain them in an inventory such as a Software Bill of Materials (SBOM).

## Section 9 — A.7.4 Physical Security Monitoring
<!-- meta: section_id=S9, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=false, cross_refs=[], keywords=["physical security","intrusion detection","video surveillance","alarm system"], applies_to=["it_security","compliance_officer"] -->

### Purpose
Control A.7.4 protects all of an organization's premises — from factory floor to office
building to company grounds — ensuring equipment, trade secrets, and employees are safe
from physical intruders. Per the German "Wirtschaftsschutz 2023" study, theft of IT and
telecommunications equipment tops the list of physical security incidents: 35% of
organizations have experienced it, and another 32% expect it to happen.

### Implementation requirements

- Use surveillance systems — intrusion detection systems, video surveillance, or security
  guards — for continuous monitoring of physical premises, performed internally or via a
  service provider.
- Seamlessly monitor premises with critical systems, typically requiring video
  surveillance that records access both inside and outside the sensitive area.
- Install contact detectors on windows, doors, or objects to trigger a panic alarm on
  contact or interruption; install infrared/acoustic sensors to detect movement or
  unusual noises (e.g. a broken windowpane). Install these on all exterior doors and
  accessible windows.
- Keep information about the surveillance infrastructure strictly confidential.
- Protect surveillance systems from unauthorized access — both to surveillance data (e.g.
  camera feeds) and against possible deactivation.
- Ensure the alarm control panel and detectors are tamper-proof and regularly tested,
  especially battery-powered components. Locate the central alarm control panel in an
  alarm-protected area.
- Use monitoring systems in compliance with applicable data protection laws, with
  particular attention to personnel monitoring and video retention periods.

## Section 11 — Conclusion and Recertification Timeline
<!-- meta: section_id=S11, is_definitions=false, keep_whole=false, cross_refs=["S1"], keywords=["recertification","mandatory","deadline","October 2025"] -->

The revision of ISO/IEC 27001:2022 marks an important milestone for information security
and the reliability of corresponding management systems, in light of increasing
geopolitical tensions and accelerating digitization.

Certification against the updated 2022 version has been mandatory since May 1, 2024.
Existing certificates issued against the older 2013 version of the standard cease to be
valid at the end of October 31, 2025. Organizations should address the changed
requirements early in order to make timely adjustments to their ISMS and ensure successful
recertification, particularly where specialized personnel resources are limited.
